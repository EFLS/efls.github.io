Emacs anniversary

Or how I accidentally learned Emacs and Vim at the same time

Each september is my “Emacs anniversary”. It feels like I’m a sort of addict when put this way, but: I’ve been using Emacs since september 2017.

Time to reflect on the journey. But before mentioning my first encounter with Emacs (which ultimately took over most of my “software needs”), let me take a step back.

I don’t have much of a “technical” background, but I had an interest in IT. Writing my masters’ thesis in Sociology with Word was something I found very frustrating, so when I started on my PhD I started explroing different types of software for writing. It started with Scrivener (writing software for Mac), but I soon realised that I was more interested in plain text writing. This lead me to Ulysses, a markdown editor that made it easy to manage multiple markdown files. (I kept using Ulysses to manage my plain text fieldwork notes during my PhD.) Writing in markdown was nice, so I moved to Atom1 to write text files and export these with pandoc to PDF and html. Atom was a nice encounter with “customizability”, as it was pretty configurable thanks to third-party packages.

Around september 2017 I encountered Spacemacs. Although I can’t remember how it crossed my path – must have read about it “somewhere on the internet” – it immediately interested me. Spacemacs came with clear installation instructions and included a tutorial that I followed. When I first launched the program, I picked the default “Vim” keybindings the configuration wizard suggested. I had never heard of “Vim” or “Emacs” style keybindings.

So yeah, it took me a while to figure out both at the same time: Vim motions and Emacs concepts (and some additional time to figure out what is exactly the distinction between the two).

The first weeks and months of using Emacs, while I was still only exploring its use, it took some effort to get around, but thanks to the included tutorial and Spacemacs’ excellent discoverability I persisted.2 Even a year or two later, after I moved from Spacemacs to my own Emacs configuration, I used “Evil” keybindings for a few years (before switching to “vanilla” keybindings, which I’m currently using).

While I wouldn’t recommend anyone to learn Emacs while also learning Vim movements and keybindings at the same time, doing so was very rewarding.

It even turns out I liked (and maybe stil like) the idea of modal editing, especially for prose. Switching to a different mode to move the cursor in only one or two keystrokes, and then delete and replace text… That’s a convenient writing environment, because editing text is a major ingredient of writing. I often miss the “feel” of modal editing, but am now very comfortable with Emacs vanilla keybindings. And after all, holding C- or M- for a few keystrokes, that’s also pretty modal.

Footnotes:

1

Atom, a (to me at least) promising editor that by now is discontinued! Something that’s very difficult to imagine happening to Emacs.

2

The Spacemacs website reads “ergonomics, mnemonics and consistency” – an apt description. It should include: a great entry point to Emacs (although I understand why it doesn’t).
